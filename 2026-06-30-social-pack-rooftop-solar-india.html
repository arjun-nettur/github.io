Rooftop solar subsidies in India have gotten more generous than ever — so why is adoption still slower than the targets suggest? 🌞

It's almost never the panels. It's the layer of paperwork, cash flow timing, and trust sitting on top of them.

Here's what's actually slowing things down, and how households and businesses are working around it:

📌 The subsidy doesn't remove the upfront cost — PM Surya Ghar offers up to ₹78,000 off, but it's usually disbursed AFTER installation and inspection. You still have to front the full amount first.

📌 DISCOM approval is the real bottleneck — Net metering needs a feasibility study, site inspection, and meter installation. Some states clear this in weeks. Others take months — partly because net metering quietly cuts into DISCOM revenue.

📌 Apartments face a structural problem — Roof ownership and electricity consumption are split across multiple flats, so group net metering policies are still immature in most states.

📌 Dust can cut output by up to 25% — Especially in northern and western India. Regular panel cleaning isn't optional, it's part of the ROI.

📌 Not everyone has to own the system — Under the RESCO model, a developer installs and owns the panels on your roof, and you just pay a lower per-unit tariff. No upfront cost at all. Common for businesses and institutions.

The honest takeaway: the technology has been ready for years. What's catching up now is the system around it — financing, approvals, and trust in installers.

India's rooftop solar subsidy can cover up to ₹78,000 per household — yet adoption still trails the 500GW renewable target by a wide margin.

The gap isn't technological. It's operational — financing structure, DISCOM execution speed, and installer trust.

Here's what you need to know:

→ Most subsidy schemes disburse AFTER installation and inspection, not upfront — meaning the consumer still has to front the full system cost first.
→ Net metering approval is the single biggest delay point. DISCOMs vary widely in turnaround, and some are financially disincentivized from approving it quickly since it reduces revenue from high-paying consumers.
→ The CAPEX model (self-owned) dominates residential adoption, but commercial and institutional users are increasingly shifting to RESCO — a third party owns the system, the building owner just pays a fixed, below-market tariff.
→ Apartment buildings remain underserved — group net metering policy is still immature across most Indian states, limiting solar access for anyone who doesn't own an independent rooftop.
→ Maintenance is not a footnote — dust accumulation alone can reduce panel output by 15-25% without a regular cleaning schedule.

The real signal here: financing innovation (RESCO, leasing, aggregator-led rollouts) is now doing more to move adoption forward than the subsidy headlines.
